Susquehanna vs. Schuylkill: The Tale of Two Funny Rivers

{{date}}

The trip from Maryland to Pennsylvania saw us see two rivers with particularly unique names, the Susquehanna River and the Schuylkill River. The type of names one would never forget. Definitely more noticable than Snake River. Of course, behind every name is an equally fascinating Where do these names come from, and which one is better? Here are my thoughts.

Driving pass Havre De Grace is the Susquehanna River, which empties in the Chesapeake Bay and goes well into Pennsylvania, 464 miles in length before reaching its source in Upstate New York. Pronounced Suskwehanna, the name is from a Delaware Indian term meaning Oyster River. As it turns out, Oyster beds were found near the mouth of the river, leading to its name.

On the flip side is Schuylkill River, a tributary of the Delaware River, which flows 135 miles from the Delaware to Upstate Pennsylvania. Pronounced School-kill, The name comes from a Dutch term meaning Hidden Creek. This was maybe because of the river's confluence with the Delaware River that was hidden by dense vegetation.

There are certainly many reasons to enjoy the names Susquehanna and Schuylkill, despite that fact that each River's name is easier to misspell. As it is, I can misspell Schulykill, Schulkyill or Schulkill all from Schuylkill. Of course, there's also Suquehanna, Susquhanna or Susquehana. In a perfect world, we'd have Susqueschuylkillhanna or Schuylsquesukillhanna or Sukillhannaqueschuyl. But at the end of the day, we have Susquehanna and Schuylkill, both located in Pennsylvania within one hour apart.
