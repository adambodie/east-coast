After the Trip: 4:44 Review

While in New York, I saw plenty of ads promoting JAY-Z's upcoming album, 4:44. Labeled at the time a Tidal/Sprint exclusive (for those of you who don't know, Tidal is JAY-Z's music streaming service), I had no intention of buying the album. I figured it would eventually be released on other platforms and available to purchase, not stream, on iTunes. He did similar with his previous album, Magna Carta Holy Grail, an album I couldn't get into. A week after June 30th, JAY-Z followed through and released his album for all.

After previewing the album, and reading about the early reviews, I gave JAY-Z a second chance. Magna Carta Holy Grail seemed uninteresting and too artsy. JAY-Z had reached billionaire status and had nothing to fight for. He was enjoying the high light with no conflict. A far cry from Reasonable Doubt and The Blueprint. But as we saw in the past couple of years in JAY-Z's life, being at the top is hard to maintain. With among other things, a public spat with his wife Beyonce's accusations of infidelity, JAY-Z had something to struggle with, to fight for.

4:44, JAY-Z's 13th album, is his first album with production credits from one producer. That producer is No I.D., who among other things, was behind Common's early works. A more soulful producer than JAY-Z's accustomed to, JAY-Z finds himself with a far different outlook on life than with previous works.

That is true on the first single, and arguably the standout of the album, "The Story of O.J.", where JAY-Z finds himself rapping about being rich as an African American. Over a bluesy beat, JAY-Z couldn't find himself in a more non-JAY-Z like beat, but manages to spit rhymes as fresh as those from Reasonable Doubt. JAY-Z, who admitted he intended to rhyme about subjects never mentioned before, spits on the title track (4:44) about his infidelity, apologizing for it and fighting to win the love of Beyonce and daughter Blue. A grown man is able to admit their mistakes, and JAY-Z uses this album to do so. He also takes the opportunity to showcase his appreciation for the LGBT community with the help of his mother, Gloria, who came out of the closet on the track "Smile."

JAY-Z still has the capability of coming hard on a track. That much is shown on the first song, Kill JAY-Z, where he subliminally disses former friend Kanye West, and "Caught Their Eyes", where he takes aim at Prince's manager for reneging on a music deal he had with Prince before his passing. Mixed in the album is JAY-Z flexing his lyrical muscles, while flaunting his abilities to rhyme "with no pen."

JAY-Z had seen his up and downs in his career, from his mafioso influenced debut Reasonable Doubt, to his more pop-oriented albums like Vol. 2 Hard Knock Life. But after 21 years in the game, JAY-Z has proven he can still be a master lyricist. No matter what path JAY-Z has taken, he still remains clearly above his peers in not only his lyrics, but his business savvy that has kept him above most. It's true, I wrote him off after Magna Carta Holy Grail, but JAY-Z proved me wrong. He can still experience emotional pain and rhyme about it with such precision, all while providing the perfect backdrop to his madness. In all, 4:44 showcases JAY-Z in a different light, a human being, and that makes this one of his better albums. JAY-Z knows how to prove people wrong, and he certainly did that here.
